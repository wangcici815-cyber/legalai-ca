Back in 2022, Pasadena voters passed Measure H — the city's big rent control package. It capped rent increases on older apartments, added just-cause eviction protections, and created a rental housing board.

Turns out, parts of it went too far.

In December 2025, the California Court of Appeal cut out two major pieces of the law in *California Apartment Assn. v. City of Pasadena*. Here's what changed and what didn't.

## What got struck down

**Relocation assistance for single-family homes and condos.** The original law said if you're renting a house or condo in Pasadena and your landlord raises the rent past a certain point, forcing you to move, the landlord has to pay your relocation costs. The court said that conflicts with Costa-Hawkins — a state law that exempts single-family homes and condos from rent control. You can't skip rent control on those properties but still slap financial penalties on landlords who raise rents on them. That's preemption.

**Extra notice before eviction.** Measure H required landlords to give tenants a "notice to cease" before filing an eviction for non-payment of rent. Basically, a warning shot before the formal notice. The court said that's also preempted by state law. California's eviction process is spelled out in the Code of Civil Procedure — your 3-day notice, your unlawful detainer lawsuit, your court hearing. Cities can't add extra procedural steps on top of that.

## What survived

A fair amount, actually. The court left in place:

- **Rent caps for pre-1995 multifamily buildings** — the 2.25% annual increase cap (adjustable based on inflation) stays.
- **Just-cause eviction protections** — you still can't be evicted without a valid reason.
- **Rental registration requirements** — landlords still have to register their units.
- **Relocation assistance for no-fault evictions** — if you're evicted through no fault of your own (the landlord wants to move in, or take the unit off the market), you still get relocation help.
- **The tenant-majority Rental Housing Board** — that's still operational.

## What this means if you're a tenant in Pasadena

Your basic rent control protections are still in place. Your landlord still can't jack up the rent on an older apartment beyond the cap, and they still need a valid reason to evict you. But those extra tenant-friendly add-ons — the warning notice, the relocation payments for SFH tenants priced out by rent increases — are gone.

If you're in a single-family home or condo, you're now in the same position as tenants in the rest of the state: no rent cap (under Costa-Hawkins), no special relocation rights tied to rent increases. You're still protected by state-level just-cause eviction rules under AB 1482, assuming your tenancy qualifies.

## The bigger picture

This is part of a pattern. Cities across California keep trying to expand tenant protections beyond what state law allows, and courts keep trimming them back. The same thing happened in Los Angeles in early 2026 with that city's relocation assistance ordinance.

The message from the courts is pretty consistent: cities can add tenant protections, but they can't interfere with the state's established eviction process or impose costs on landlords for raising rent on units that state law says are exempt from rent control.

Costa-Hawkins is still the 900-pound gorilla in the room, and until the legislature or voters change it, local rent control efforts are going to keep running into the same wall.
