If you're divorced in California and your child support order includes a "bonus formula" — meaning your ex gets a percentage of any bonus you earn on top of base support — there's a recent case you should know about.

In January 2026, a California appeals court handed down *Bauer v. Bauer*. The ruling settled something that's been fuzzy for a while: when you sign one of those agreements, does a regular salary raise count the same as a bonus?

The court's answer: no, it doesn't.

## The case, briefly

The Bauers had a divorce judgment that included what's called an "Ostler-Smith" provision — that's legalese for a table that says how much of each parent's bonus goes toward additional child and spousal support, on top of the base amount.

The problem started when the ex-husband got a regular salary increase at work. His ex-wife argued that the raise should be included in the Ostler-Smith calculation, meaning she'd get a cut of it. The trial court agreed with her.

The appeals court didn't.

The judges said the bonus table was designed to handle "fluctuating, discretionary income" — things like performance bonuses, commissions, or stock payouts. A regular salary bump, by contrast, is predictable and permanent. If the agreement doesn't specifically say that salary increases go into the bonus formula, then they don't.

## Why this matters

A lot of California divorce decrees include Ostler-Smith provisions. They're common in cases where one parent works in an industry with big variable pay — sales, tech, entertainment, medicine. The idea is to capture upside without having to go back to court every year.

But what qualifies as "upside" matters. If your decree says "10% of any bonus over $10,000" but doesn't mention salary increases, and you get a $20,000 raise, this ruling says that raise is off the table.

On the flip side, if you're the spouse receiving support and your ex keeps getting raises that their bonus provision doesn't touch, you might want to look at modifying the base support order instead.

## The practical takeaway

If you're going through a divorce right now and negotiating one of these provisions, be specific. Don't let the agreement say "additional compensation" or "bonus" without defining it. Spell out: does this cover salary increases? Stock grants? Restricted stock units? Commissions? Signing bonuses?

If you already have an order with vague language, this case could work in your favor or against you depending on which side you're on. Worth running by a lawyer to see if it affects your situation.

The case is *Bauer v. Bauer*, filed January 12, 2026 in the Second Appellate District. You can read the full opinion [here](https://www.courtlistener.com/opinion/10773299/bauer-v-bauer-ca26/).
